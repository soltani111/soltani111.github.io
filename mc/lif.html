One Color LIF Method for Temperature Measurement

Introduction

In this project, we used the one-color Laser-Induced Fluorescence (LIF) method to calculate the temperature of a fluid. This method leverages the fluorescent properties of certain particles, which, when excited by laser light at a specific wavelength, emit radiation in a particular range of electromagnetic waves. The intensity of this emitted light is dependent on temperature, allowing us to determine the fluid's temperature. This temperature dependence varies across different fluorescent materials; for most, the output radiation intensity increases as the temperature decreases.

Test Theory

The quantum efficiency \(Q\) plays a central role in this experiment and is influenced by several parameters:

  • \(K_{\text{spec}}\) is the intensity of fluorescent radiation received by the camera.
  • \(K_{\text{opt}}\) is the optical efficiency due to optical equipment.
  • \(\epsilon_1\) and \(\epsilon_2\) are the absorption coefficients of the fluorescent material and laser light, respectively.
  • \(C\) is the concentration of the fluorescent material.
  • \(I_0\) is the intensity of the incoming laser light.
  • \(b\) and \(z\) are the lengths of the paths traveled by the beam in the container and by the fluorescent radiation perpendicular to the laser sheet, respectively.

Given these parameters, the light emitted by the fluorescent materials is dependent on the temperature. This relationship is expressed by the equation:

\[ \frac{I_f}{I_{f_{\text{ref}}}} = e^{\beta\left(\frac{1}{T} - \frac{1}{T_0}\right)} \]

where:

  • \(I_f\) is the fluorescence intensity in the test mode.
  • \(I_{f_{\text{ref}}}\) is the fluorescence intensity in the reference mode.
  • \(\beta\) is a coefficient determined experimentally.
  • \(T\) and \(T_0\) are the test and reference temperatures, respectively.

When the temperature is constant but the concentration changes, the relationship is given by:

\[ \frac{I_f}{I_{f_{\text{ref}}}} = \frac{C}{C_{\text{ref}}} \]

This method assumes a uniform concentration of fluorescent particles throughout the container and low fluctuations of laser light intensity.

Test Equipment

The experimental setup involved creating a light sheet in the test container using a laser and a cylindrical lens. A camera, placed perpendicular to the laser sheet, captured images of the fluorescent emissions. The fluorescent substance used was Rhodamine B, a powder dissolved in water to produce a solution of known concentration. A filter was placed on the camera to ensure only light emitted by the fluorescent particles was captured, excluding any laser light interference. All experiments were conducted in a dark room to minimize the influence of external light sources.

Description of Experiment

  1. Preparation of Fluorescent Solution:
    • Dissolve Rhodamine particles in water.
    • Stir with a magnetic stirrer until the solution becomes uniform.
    • Determine the solution concentration, which serves as the reference concentration.
  2. Concentration Measurement:
    • Inject a concentrated solution of Rhodamine into a container with a dilute solution using a syringe.
    • Capture images to record the fluorescent emissions.
    • Calculate concentration at any point using the images and reference data.
  3. Temperature Measurement:
    • Stir the uniform solution with a magnetic stirrer and measure the initial temperature (26°C). Take reference images.
    • Heat the solution to 53°C while stirring continues to ensure uniform temperature distribution.
    • Capture images at both temperatures to develop a calibration curve.
    • Inject cold fluid into the container and take additional images to determine the temperature distribution using the calibration curve and experimental data.

By analyzing these images and employing the calibration curve, we accurately mapped the temperature distribution within the fluid.
